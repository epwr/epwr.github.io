I believe that the three biggest problems our society will face in the next 30 years are: <a href="#climate">climate change</a>, <a href="#democracy">weak democratic institutions</a>, and the <a href="#economy">bifurcation of wealth</a>.



<h3 id="climate">Climate change</h3>

We're on track to blow by the 1.5 degrees of warming target from the [Paris Agreement](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/). The direct impact of climate change will be horrible for some: homes will be destroyed by fires, people will die during extreme heat when they wouldn't have without it, and crops will fail.

The second order impact that we aren't talking about is that:

> Climate change will increase the real cost of all physical goods sold by 20-40%.

These costs will come from a few different areas:

- The cost to transport goods will increase. We will need to invest in increasing the capacity of our transport networks by 2-5% because extreme weather will disrupt the network more frequently, and we'll need more capacity to move the same amount of goods each year. We'll be able to measure this by seeing the cost to the shipper go down due to technology gains, but the cost to the user stay high due to the need for the shipper to build in the cost of this increased capacity.

- The cost of insurance for physical locations will increase by 10% on average.

<h3 id="noise">Noise</h3>

The following topics are things we will likely need to manage, but they shouldn't be viewed as fundemental issues:

<h4>Immigration</h4>

Every one is worried about stressed local systems. Where I live this is (currently) not enough doctors, and high housing costs.

Limiting immigration won't help these issues. We need strong democratic institutions to be able to plan long term and invest in building housing / training doctors for the predicted demand in 10-20 years. Having higher demand just means we need more building, but housing rate is delayed by X years behind the funding, so let's pick the growth rate we want & fund for that.
